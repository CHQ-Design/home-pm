# Toft vs Toftly — Head-to-Head

_Prepared: 2026-05-19. Comparison between the working name (Toft) and the proposed -ly derivative (Toftly)._

## Defensibility (preliminary screen only)

**Toft:** Passes. Active uses are a Danish .NET consultancy, an IT services consultancy, one Google Play app in field-services category, an open-source GitHub library, and an industrial instrument tool. Scandinavian-surname noise is real but not a brand-collision issue. Trademark space appears open in consumer-app classes. See `toft-maru-screening.md`.

**Toftly:** Passes more cleanly. The only "Toftly" surfaced is a small UK bakery in York ("Toftly Treats"). No software products, no app-store listings, no SaaS brands. Coined words tend to be presumptively distinctive in trademark filings — easier to register, easier to defend.

**On defensibility alone, Toftly wins.** It's the cleaner namespace.

## What each name does as a brand

The two names point in materially different brand directions, and that matters more than the defensibility delta:

**Toft is a real word with real meaning.** Old English / Old Norse for a homestead with its surrounding plot of land. The name _means_ what the product is for. When a user learns it, they gain something. The brand can grow into the etymology. This is the same trick Stripe, Patch, and Field Notes pull — short, real, slightly old, real meaning intact.

**Toftly is a coined word in a recognized branding pattern.** Bitly (2008), Calendly (2013), Grammarly (2009), Embedly (2012), Casually, Surely, Otterly, Stonly, dozens of others. The "-ly" suffix is a well-understood SaaS-naming convention — users see it and immediately read "brand name, look it up". The brand defines the meaning rather than the word carrying it.

Neither is wrong. They're different strategies.

## Where each name wins

### Toftly wins on

- **Defensibility.** Cleaner trademark surface; less search-result noise.
- **Recognition.** Users see "-ly" and know it's a brand. No "what does this word mean?" cognitive load on first encounter.
- **Pronunciation.** Unambiguous — "toft" + "lee". Toft alone might get mispronounced on first read.
- **App-store / SEO disambiguation.** Searching "Toftly" returns only us; searching "Toft" returns the Danish consultancy, the field-services app, the surname GitHub profiles, etc.
- **Lower brand-build cost.** Less explanatory work on the login screen, App Store description, install prompt.

### Toft wins on

- **Tone fit.** The current UI is editorial / warm / household-journal — Fraunces serif wordmark, "Things" / "Routines" / "Projects" / "Notes" page titles, "Add a thought above" empty state, "6 things handled" completed-section label. Toft sets crisply in this register. Toftly leans toward SaaS-startup aesthetic in a way that may fight the existing voice.
- **Real meaning intact.** Toft means what the product is for; Toftly's meaning is whatever we define. Real meaning is a brand asset that compounds — every time a user learns it, the brand earns trust.
- **Era-resistance.** "-ly" names date themselves to the 2010s SaaS wave. A 2026 launch under a "-ly" name will feel era-marked to a 2030 user. Toft has no era signal — it could have been founded in 1925 or 2025.
- **Differentiation from category.** The household-app category (Cozi, Hearth, Maple, Homsy, OurHome, Sweepy, Tody, Stead-Bristol) is already crowded. Toft signals "not another family-organizer app" — its quiet weight reads as a different kind of product. Toftly puts the brand back into the family-organizer bucket aesthetically.
- **Wordmark.** Toft is 4 chars, 1 syllable. Crisper in the small top-nav slot. Toftly is 6 chars and the descender-heavy ending (`-ftly`) reads less weighty in Fraunces.
- **Higher ceiling.** Toft is a strategic name (more risk, more memorable, more brand-defining). Toftly is a tactical name (less risk, less standout, blends into a known pattern).

## My recommendation

**Toft, with intention.**

The current product has already chosen its aesthetic axis: editorial-warm with cute moments at the edges (kid-mode emojis, streak counter), not cute-SaaS with editorial moments. The wordmark sets in Fraunces. The H1 reads "Things" in serif. The empty state reads "Add a thought above". That brand already exists in the product — Toft fits it; Toftly fights it. Choosing Toftly would force a brand pivot, which is a real cost on top of the rename itself.

The cost of Toft — that users have to be taught the word — is real but solvable. Three places where it gets resolved cheaply:

1. **First-run onboarding line.** Something like _"Toft. Old word for the land around a home. New app for what needs doing on yours."_ One sentence, lands the pronunciation, lands the meaning, lands the positioning.
2. **Footer or about page.** A two-sentence etymology note. Users who care will read it; users who don't will just remember the name.
3. **Wordmark presentation.** Keep "Toft" in Fraunces. The serif typography signals "old word, on purpose" rather than "we couldn't think of a real name". The brand owns the unfamiliarity rather than hiding from it.

That's the playbook Stripe used ("Stripe is a noun for a band of color, and a verb for marking with bands"), that Patch used, that several quietly successful brands have used. It works specifically because the typography and voice signal _intent_, not accident.

## When I'd switch to Toftly

- If the brand-build budget is genuinely thin and Toft's explanatory cost feels unmanageable.
- If you want to position the app explicitly in the consumer-SaaS family-organizer category (not against it).
- If audience research surfaces that "weird old word" reads as "weird and confusing" to your target user rather than "interesting and considered".
- If app-store and SEO search-result clarity matters more than any other brand consideration.

These are real conditions. None of them are currently true for home-pm based on what's in the code, but they could become true.

## Caveat

Both names still need attorney-led USPTO TESS searches and domain/social-handle availability checks before commercial commitment. Preliminary screening is necessary, not sufficient.
