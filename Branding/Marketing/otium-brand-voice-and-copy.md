## The brand in one paragraph

Otium is the Latin word for productive leisure — the quiet, attended-to hours of life that aren't work but still matter. The Roman concept specifically contrasted *otium* with *negotium* (work/business): otium was the hours of contemplation, conversation, and the things one chose to do well. For a household-management app, this is almost a one-line product brief. The home is otium territory. Otium runs the work of home without making it feel like work.

The brand voice should sound like a calm housemate who already has things handled. Quiet competence. Warm but never twee. Confident without being loud. Dry humor in reserve.

---

## Brand voice — five bullets

- **Sound like:** A calm, warm household — competent and kind. The voice works for a seven-year-old reading their chore list and a parent running the day. Confident, warm, quietly proud of work done.
- **Sentence rhythm:** Short. One idea per line. Periods do most of the work. An exclamation point lands when something genuinely earns it — a kid checking off their first chore, a clear-week milestone, the day's work all done. Use them like spice, not seasoning.
- **Recognize work, every time.** Otium is a household app, not an editorial brand. When something gets done, the app should say so — warmly, briefly, like a parent in passing. *"Nice work." "All done — go play." "That one's been waiting a while."* This is how a household feels alive.
- **Don't sound like:** Corporate productivity software ("optimize," "streamline," "boost," "supercharge," "unlock"). Gym-bro motivational ("you got this!" "crush it!" "level up!"). Grown-up condescension toward kids ("good job, sport!" "you're a superstar!"). Cottagecore preciousness ("cozy little tasks!"). Latin-professor lecturing.
- **One sentence that's on-voice:** *"Six things done. Nice work, house."*

## The pronunciation question (and its solution)

Otium is pronounced **OH-tee-um** in standard English (closer to OH-tee-oom in classical Latin, but English speakers will land on OH-tee-um and that's fine).

This is non-obvious to most users. The solution is the same one Toft was going to use: teach it cheaply, once, in three places, then never mention it again.

**Three places to teach pronunciation:**

1. **First-run onboarding line** — woven into the welcome screen
2. **Footer/About page** — full one-paragraph etymology note
3. **App Store / Google Play description** — first line includes the pronunciation

Don't put a pronunciation guide on every screen. Don't repeat the etymology. The brand should signal *"old word, on purpose"* through typography (Fraunces serif) and quiet confidence, not by over-explaining itself.

## In-app copy recommendations

### App title / wordmark

**Otium** — set in Fraunces serif. Two syllables, three letters of weight on either side of a long "i." The wordmark should breathe. Generous letter-spacing. No tagline locked under the wordmark.

### Tagline (lead candidate and alternates)

**Lead:**
> *Otium. The quiet hours, attended to.*

**Alternates worth voice-testing:**
> *Otium. The work of home, quietly handled.*
> *Otium. Run your household without the noise.*
> *Otium. The household, in a good rhythm.*
> *Otium. What needs doing — calmly.*

The lead tagline plays on the word's own etymology (otium = the quiet hours) and the product's job (attended to = the household-task-management work). Both halves earn their keep.

### Onboarding sequence

**Screen 1 — Welcome:**
> *Welcome to Otium.*
> *(OH-tee-um. An old Latin word for the quiet hours when meaningful things happen.)*
> [Continue]

**Screen 2 — What it does:**
> *Add what needs doing.*
> *Check it off when it's done.*
> *The rest is yours.*
> [Continue]

**Screen 3 — Set up the house:**
> *Who lives here?*
> [Add household member] [Add another] [Done]

**Screen 4 — First thing:**
> *Add the first thing.*
> *Anything from "buy milk" to "find a plumber."*
> [Text field: "What needs doing?"]
> [Add]

### Primary CTAs (use these verbatim)

- **Add task:** `Add to today` (not "Add task" — that's the productivity-app default; *to today* makes it feel like placing something on a shelf rather than logging into a system)
- **Mark done:** `Done` (not "Complete," not "Mark as completed" — just `Done`)
- **Add household member:** `Add someone`
- **Edit list:** `Tidy this list`
- **Settings:** `Adjust`
- **Save:** `Keep` or `Save it`
- **Cancel:** `Never mind`

### Empty states

**No tasks anywhere (first-time empty):**
> *Nothing here yet. Add the first thing.*

**Today is empty:**
> *Nothing on today. Enjoy the quiet.*

**Today is empty after work was done — celebrate it:**
> *All done! Six things handled. The day is yours.*
> *(swap "Six" for the actual count; the exclamation earns its keep here)*

**Kid-facing version of the same:**
> *All done! Nice work. Go play.*

**This week is empty:**
> *Clear week ahead.*

**Search returned nothing:**
> *Nothing matches. Try a different word?*

**Network error / offline:**
> *Otium can't reach the house just now. Try again in a moment.*

### Navigation labels

Lead pattern (matches the existing voice):

> **Today · This Week · All · The House**

- **Today** — what's on for the day
- **This Week** — upcoming
- **All** — everything
- **The House** — household members, recurring tasks, settings

Alternates if any feel awkward:
- "Soon" instead of "This Week"
- "Settings" or "Home" instead of "The House" (but *The House* fits the brand and avoids the home-screen ambiguity)

### PWA install prompt

> *Install Otium*
>
> *Put Otium on your home screen — it's there when you need it.*
>
> [Install] [Not now]

Alternate, shorter:
> *Install Otium — it belongs on your home screen.*

### Error and help language

- **Generic error:** *That didn't go through. Try once more?*
- **Save failed:** *Couldn't keep that. One more try?*
- **Sign-in failed:** *Couldn't sign in. Check the email and password?*
- **Sync error:** *Otium is out of sync with the house. Reconnecting…*
- **Help intro:** *Otium does one thing — keeps the household running calmly. If something feels off, write us at hi@otiumapp.com.*

### Celebrations — how Otium recognizes work done

A household app needs to recognize work — and a child-friendly app needs to do it in a way that lands for kids without being patronizing or hype-y. Otium's celebrations aren't confetti and fireworks. They're a parent saying *"nicely done"* in passing. Warm, brief, real.

**When to celebrate:**

- A task gets checked off (small, in-line acknowledgment)
- All of today's tasks are done (warmer moment, both visual and verbal)
- A kid checks off their first task ever (one-time warm milestone)
- A long-running recurring task finally gets done (recognition of consistency)
- The household completes a streak / a clear week (quiet milestone, not gamification)

**What celebration looks like in-brand:**

- **A warm sentence.** *"Nice work." "Done — that one's been waiting." "All done! Go play." "The whole list, handled."*
- **A small visual moment.** Gentle animation. A brief color flash. A strike-through that feels satisfying — the line lands with weight, not bounce.
- **An optional sound.** A quiet chime. Soft, single note, not a fanfare. Configurable in settings; off by default for adult-only households, on for households with kids per setup.
- **A high-five emoji** is fine in kid-facing celebration contexts. Deviation from the general "no emoji in product copy" rule — kids respond to them, adults don't mind them in a household app. Use sparingly: completion moments only, never in regular UI.

**What celebration never looks like:**

- Confetti explosions, cascading stars, particle effects
- Motivational quotes ("You're crushing it!" "Keep up the good work!")
- Badges, achievements, levels, XP, streaks-as-pressure
- Multiple exclamation points
- Caps lock
- Sound effects that interrupt
- Praise of the person rather than the work ("You're amazing!" → instead: "All done!")

**Praise the work, not the person.** This is the through-line for kid-friendly celebration that ages well. *"All done!"* lands at every age. *"You're a superstar!"* lands at age six and is grating at sixteen.

### Notification copy

Use sparingly. The app should feel like a calm housemate, not a needy one.

- **Daily morning summary:** *Three things on today.*
- **Task reminder:** *"[Task name]" — today.*
- **Someone added a task:** *[Name] added "[Task]" to the house.*
- **Weekly summary (optional):** *Eighteen things handled this week.*

Never:
- *Don't forget!*
- *You haven't logged in!*
- *Boost your productivity today!*
- Push notifications with emoji

## Icon and visual identity

- **Wordmark:** Fraunces serif, "Otium" with generous letter-spacing. The "O" carries the icon weight; consider a wordmark-only logotype with no separate icon mark.
- **App icon:** A Fraunces capital "O" on the editorial-warm background. The "O" has a natural roundness that signals calm. Alternative: an "O" with a single quiet "·" mark inside it (suggesting one thing attended to). Avoid: ornaments, gradients, anything cottagecore.
- **Color palette:** Keep the existing editorial-warm palette. No re-skin needed — Fraunces + warm cream / muted earth tones already carry the brand.
- **Typography hierarchy:** Fraunces for display (wordmark, headlines, taglines). A clean grotesque (Inter, Söhne, or whatever the current UI font is) for body text and interface chrome. Don't set body copy in Fraunces — it loses its editorial weight when overused.

## What NOT to ship

- A logo with a house icon
- A tagline with the word "manage" or "optimize"
- An onboarding sequence that explains the etymology in more than one screen
- Emoji scattered through regular UI copy *(emoji in kid-facing celebration moments are fine — see Celebrations section)*
- Any reference to the app's users as "users"
- Any pronunciation guide outside of the three sanctioned places (onboarding, footer, app store description)
- Confetti explosions, cascading stars, achievement-badge animations *(quiet celebration animations are encouraged — see Celebrations section)*
- Gamification scaffolding (XP, levels, achievements, leaderboards, streaks-as-pressure)
- Motivational quote overlays
- "Boost," "supercharge," "unlock," "10x," "level up," "crush it"
- "You're crushing it!" "You're a superstar!" — praise the work, not the person
- A blog post titled "How Otium will revolutionize your household"

## Voice test prompts (for Craig)

Before locking any of this, read the following sentences out loud in a normal speaking voice. If any feel embarrassing, hard, or pretentious, that signals where the brand needs adjustment:

1. *"I use Otium for the house."*
2. *"Have you tried Otium?"*
3. *"Hi, I'm calling from Otium."*
4. *"Otium. The quiet hours, attended to."*
5. *"Six things handled. Two left. The otium part starts now."*
6. *"Otium — say OH-tee-um — is a calm way to keep your household running."*

If 1–3 feel awkward, the brand name itself isn't working and we need to revisit. If 4–6 feel awkward but 1–3 are fine, the taglines need adjustment.

## Open questions

1. **Theme color / hex codes** — using the existing palette per memory; would be useful to lock specific hex values here for engineering handoff.
2. **Pronunciation in onboarding** — should it appear on screen 1 (welcome) or screen 2 (what it does)? Recommendation: screen 1, parenthetical, smaller type. Don't make it the first thing the user reads — but make sure it's the first thing they see in passing.
3. **"The House" navigation label** — does this read clearly enough vs. "Settings"? Worth user-testing if there's any cohort access. The brand argument is strong; the usability argument may push toward "Settings" for some users.
4. **Confirmation vs. celebration** — when a task is marked done, what happens visually? Recommendation: a quiet animation (line strikes through, item dims, slides off-screen after a beat). No confetti, no haptics on iOS, no sound.
5. **First-run pronunciation note language** — current draft: *"(OH-tee-um. An old Latin word for the quiet hours when meaningful things happen.)"* Is the parenthetical too long? Alternative: *"(OH-tee-um. Latin for productive leisure.)"* — shorter, but loses the warmth.
