# Naming — v3

**Date:** 2026-05-21
**Status:** Working list. No screening complete yet — names below are first-pass candidates, not vetted.
**Prior work:** [naming-v2.md](naming-v2.md), [toft-vs-toftly.md](toft-vs-toftly.md), [toft-maru-screening.md](toft-maru-screening.md), [stead-screening.md](stead-screening.md)

## Why v3 exists

Toft was committed on 2026-05-19 as the working name. On 2026-05-21 Craig surfaced a phonetic-collision risk: "toft" is essentially homophonic with "toff," the British slang for a wealthy, snobbish, out-of-touch upper-class person. For a household-management app whose entire emotional promise is *the grounded, well-run home*, getting "snobby out-of-touch" associations at launch would actively undermine positioning.

v2 was also clustered tightly in one metaphor lane: homestead / hearth / domestic architecture (Toft, Steading, Stoop, Eaves, Mantel). Craig's direction for v3 is to **pull away from home-and-hearth and lean into warm-productivity** — names that feel domestic in *temperament* (calm, competent, caring) without literally being words for parts of a house.

## Brand Readout

The app is a household task manager with editorial-warm aesthetic (Fraunces serif, "Add a thought above" / "6 things handled" voice). Evidence from prior screening docs: the product positions itself around *calmly handling what needs doing*, not around the romance of home ownership. The strongest brand angle is therefore **quiet competence** — a tool that helps a household stay on top of itself without ceremony.

Audience: adults running a household (homeowners, renters, families, couples, solo) — anyone with recurring domestic responsibilities they want to coordinate. Commercial intent confirmed for 6–12 month runway.

**Strongest brand axis for v3:** the satisfying feeling of having things handled. Less "old word for a homestead," more "the quiet rhythm of a household that works."

## Naming Strategy

**Direction: warm-utility verbs and rhythm words.** Real English words that connote attentive action, gentle momentum, or satisfying completion. Avoid:

- Old English / Old Norse nouns about land or dwellings (the v2 lane — saturated and slang-prone)
- "-ly" SaaS coinages (Toftly was already debated and felt thin)
- Domestic-architecture words (Stoop, Eaves, Mantel — feel decorative rather than functional)
- Hearth/fire/cottage language (Ember, Hearth, Cottage — overused in lifestyle brands)
- Generic productivity language (Tasks, Lists, Done, Track — undifferentiated)

The target shape: one syllable preferred, two acceptable. Real English word. Carries a verb-feel even when used as a noun. Sounds like something a calm person would say.

## Top App Name Recommendations

### Tend

**Why it works:** The most direct verb of care in English. "To tend to something" is exactly what the app helps you do. Warm, competent, universally legible. The noun form ("the day's tends") is a natural fit for the existing "6 things handled" voice. Pairs beautifully with serif typography.
**Tone:** quiet, caretaking, sure-handed
**URL ideas:** tendapp.com, gettend.com, tend.app (likely taken), usetend.com
**Risks:** Tend Dental is a well-known US chain — coexistence plausible (different category) but search/SEO competition. Short single-syllable names are also harder to trademark broadly. Verify slang: "to tend" is clean across major English markets.

### Cadence

**Why it works:** The rhythm of a household running well. Productivity-coded but warmer than "schedule" or "routine." Sounds like a real product, not a coinage. Fraunces serif treatment would land elegantly on a four-syllable word with a soft K-D-N consonant skeleton.
**Tone:** rhythmic, considered, professional-warm
**URL ideas:** cadenceapp.com, getcadence.com, cadence.house, runcadence.com
**Risks:** Cadence Design Systems is a major company in chip design — completely different industry, but a goliath in trademark search. Cadence Bank, Cadence Health, and several smaller Cadence apps exist. Trademark search needed across Class 9 / 42.

### Heyday

**Why it works:** Positive, era-evoking word meaning "peak / time of greatest success." Reframes the household from "things to do" to "the good era you're living in right now." Distinctive, hard to confuse with anything. Two syllables, easy to say. Has nostalgic-but-not-twee warmth.
**Tone:** optimistic, slightly literary, warm-confident
**URL ideas:** heydayapp.com, gethey day.com, heyday.house, useheyday.com
**Risks:** Heyday Skincare (US facial chain) is well-known and well-funded. Target also has a Heyday electronics line. Different categories, but heavy noise in search. Pronunciation/spelling are clean.

### Notch

**Why it works:** Small satisfying milestone. "One more notch." Conveys progress without grind. Concrete, sensory, masculine-coded but not aggressive. Single syllable. Pairs with "things handled" voice — every completed task is a notch.
**Tone:** crisp, satisfying, lightly handcrafted
**URL ideas:** notchapp.com, getnotch.com, notch.house, anotch.com
**Risks:** Markus "Notch" Persson (Minecraft creator) holds the cultural association — controversial figure post-2014 due to public statements. This is a real reputational risk if the brand ever gets enough scale to invite Google searches. Worth weighing carefully.

### Stride

**Why it works:** Productive momentum without urgency. "Hitting your stride" is exactly the feeling the app should produce — competent, paced, not rushed. Warm-productive sweet spot. One syllable, easy spelling, easy to say.
**Tone:** confident, paced, athletic-but-not-aggressive
**URL ideas:** strideapp.com, getstride.com, stride.house, instride.app
**Risks:** Stride Bank, Stride Health, Stride Rite (kids shoes), Stride Treglown (UK architects) — many brand uses but spread across industries. Productivity space less saturated. Trademark search needed.

### Knit

**Why it works:** "A close-knit household." Bringing things together. Warmth-coded without being cottagecore. Working verb. Three letters, one syllable. Fraunces serif lockup would be visually distinctive (short word, two ascenders).
**Tone:** quiet, weaving, communal
**URL ideas:** knitapp.com, getknit.com, knit.house, knitup.com
**Risks:** Heavy SEO pollution from knitting/yarn/craft content — search results will be muddy for years. Knit (the community-survey tool, smaller) exists. Spelling clean; pronunciation clean.

### Tally

**Why it works:** The satisfying count of things done. Productive-warm. Real word, easy to say, easy to spell. Family/household-friendly tone. "Today's tally: 6 things handled."
**Tone:** counting, friendly, light
**URL ideas:** tallyapp.com (likely taken), gettally.com, tally.house, taketally.com
**Risks:** Tally Forms (well-known form builder) and Tally (massive Indian accounting software) own most of the brand space. The slang "tally-ho" (UK fox-hunting cheer) carries slight upper-class English associations — milder than "toff" but on the same axis. Re-screen carefully.

### Spry

**Why it works:** Lively, capable, light-on-its-feet. Less common than its synonyms (nimble, brisk, agile), which gives more naming whitespace. Three letters, one syllable. Feels like a real word but reads as a brand.
**Tone:** lively, capable, slightly old-fashioned in a charming way
**URL ideas:** spryapp.com, getspry.com, spry.house, besprry.com
**Risks:** SPRY Fox (small game studio), Spry (smaller HR/benefits brands). Relatively open namespace. Slang clean across English markets. Pronunciation: "spry" rhymes with "fly" — clean but worth a pronunciation note in onboarding to head off "sprey" misreads.

### Daybook

**Why it works:** Old-fashioned word for a daily journal or ledger — a book where you record the day's doings. Carries the editorial-warm Fraunces aesthetic naturally. Productivity-coded (a book of things) but warm (the kind of book a thoughtful person keeps). Two syllables, easy.
**Tone:** literary, intentional, hand-kept
**URL ideas:** daybookapp.com, getdaybook.com, daybook.house, mydaybook.com
**Risks:** Daybook (existing journaling apps on iOS/Android — multiple). Direct category collision. May be findable in some markets but not defensible. Worth the screen anyway.

### Doable

**Why it works:** The most psychologically accurate word for what the app does — makes household work feel doable. Friendly, conversational, instantly understood. Adjective-as-noun-as-brand follows the Slack / Lyft / Stripe pattern (real words used unconventionally).
**Tone:** approachable, kind, no-nonsense
**URL ideas:** doableapp.com, getdoable.com, doable.house, sodoable.com
**Risks:** "Doable" is a common adjective — heavy SEO competition from generic usage. May be hard to trademark because it's so generic. Worth checking whether anyone's claimed it as a product name.

### Throughline

**Why it works:** Narrative term meaning the connecting thread that runs through a story. Reframes household management as the ongoing story of a home rather than a pile of tasks. Distinctive, literary, productivity-adjacent. Three syllables — longer than most candidates but compensated by uniqueness.
**Tone:** considered, narrative, slightly elevated
**URL ideas:** throughlineapp.com, getthroughline.com, throughline.house
**Risks:** NPR has a popular podcast called *Throughline* — significant cultural footprint, though different category. Length may be a downside for the wordmark. Some smaller productivity brands have used it.

### Crew

**Why it works:** Your household crew. The people (and tasks) you're running things with. Warm + functional. Carries the "team" feeling without being a workplace word. Could anchor copy like "the crew has 6 things on deck."
**Tone:** team-spirited, warm, casual-competent
**URL ideas:** crewapp.com, getcrew.com, crew.house, runcrew.com
**Risks:** Crew (acquired by Slack, 2019 — defunct but the name carries memory) and J.Crew (clothing) dominate the brand recall. Crew is also a Square product. Probably the most cluttered namespace on this list.

## Best 3 Recommendations

### 1. Heyday

The single most distinctive name on the list. Reframes the entire product emotionally: not "manage your chores" but "you're in a heyday." That positioning beats every "homestead/hearth" name on the v2 list because it makes the household feel *alive and current* rather than *traditional and inherited*. Pronunciation, spelling, and search-distinctiveness are all clean. Heyday Skincare is the main collision and is a category away. The risk profile is manageable, and the brand ceiling is high.

### 2. Tend

The most precise word for what the app does. The least clever name on the list, and that's its strength — it sits comfortably in the same tradition as Notion, Things, Linear, Stripe (real words used with conviction). Pairs uniquely well with Fraunces serif and with the existing "6 things handled" voice. The Tend Dental conflict is real but survivable. If Craig wants a name the app could grow into for decades, Tend is the safest long-haul pick.

### 3. Cadence

The most "professional-warm" of the candidates. Carries a rhythm-of-the-household meaning that's productivity-coded without being grindy. Sounds like a real product name in the way Linear and Notion do. The Cadence Design Systems conflict is large but in a totally different vertical. If Craig wants a name that sounds confidently established from day one — like the app has already been around a while — Cadence does that work.

**Honorable mentions worth not eliminating:** Stride (clean, athletic-warm), Daybook (Fraunces-perfect aesthetic), Knit (warmest of the short verbs).

**Knocked out from final consideration:** Crew (namespace too crowded), Tally (carries faint "tally-ho" upper-class echo — same axis as the toff risk, milder but present), Notch (Minecraft creator association is a permanent reputational shadow), Spry (good but lacks Heyday's distinctive emotional payload), Doable (likely undefendable as a generic adjective), Throughline (too long, NPR collision).

## URL / Domain Recommendations

Pattern preferences (apply to the final 1–2 candidates):

- **Exact match `.com`** — try first; almost certainly taken for any single-word real-English candidate
- **`get[name].com`** — strong fallback (getheyday.com, gettend.com, getcadence.com)
- **`[name]app.com`** — the toftapp.com playbook; clear PWA signaling
- **`use[name].com`** — Stripe/Notion era pattern
- **`[name].app`** — Google Registry TLD, signals app-ness; check at registrar not who.is (v2 lesson)
- **`[name].house`** — category-appropriate alt TLD; available more often than .com
- **`[name].co`** — clean alt to .com but signals startup-y

Skip `.io` (developer-coded, off-brand for a household app) and `.xyz` (off-brand entirely).

**Do not claim availability for any of these without verification at a real registrar (GoDaddy, Namecheap, Cloudflare).**

## Brand Voice Guidance

- **Sound like:** a calm housemate who has things handled. Confident, warm, dry humor in reserve.
- **Avoid:** corporate productivity speak ("optimize," "streamline," "boost"); cottagecore preciousness ("cozy little tasks!"); exclamation points; emoji in product copy; the word "users."
- **Sentence length:** short. One idea per line. Periods do most of the work.
- **Voice example:** *"Six things handled. Two left. The afternoon is yours."*
- **Forbidden phrases:** "let's get started," "you got this," "way to go," "achievement unlocked."

## Final Recommendation

**Lead with Heyday for distinctiveness, hold Tend as the safe second pick.** Heyday reframes the product emotionally in a way none of the v2 names did — it makes the household feel *current and good* rather than *historical and inherited*. Tend is the safer, more literal pick; if Heyday fails attorney pass or fails Craig's gut, Tend should be the fallback.

**Practical next steps:**

1. **Slang screen the top 3** (Heyday, Tend, Cadence) — formally check British, Australian, and US slang dictionaries plus Urban Dictionary. This is the step that failed for Toft. **Do this before anything else.**
2. **Voice-test the top 3** — say each name in three sentences out loud: *"I use [name] for the house," "Have you tried [name]?", "Hi, I'm calling from [name]."* If any one feels embarrassing in any of the three, drop it.
3. **Registrar check** — verify `.com`, `.app`, and `.house` for each top-3 name at a real registrar (not who.is).
4. **Handle check** — confirm `@heydayapp` / `@usetend` / `@cadenceapp` (or chosen pattern) across X, Instagram, TikTok, Threads, Bluesky, GitHub.
5. **Attorney pass** — USPTO TESS (Class 9 + 42), UKIPO, EUIPO. Cadence Design Systems is the biggest known shadow for Cadence; Heyday Skincare and Tend Dental are the main shadows for the other two.
6. **Do not** start codebase rename until steps 1–5 clear.

Until then, external references stay "The Board (working title)" per the existing convention.
