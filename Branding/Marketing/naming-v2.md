# Naming — Round 2

_Prepared: 2026-05-19. Follow-up to `branding-readout.md` after three preliminary screens disqualified Stead, Mantel, and Folio (see `stead-screening.md` and `mantel-folio-screening.md`)._

## What changed

Round 1 generated short, evocative, single-word English nouns anchored to home/household metaphors. All three top candidates collided with active products: Stead (direct same-category competitor), Mantel (celebrity-founded consumer app), Folio (six-plus active products including two consumer apps in the App Store). The pattern is clear — the namespace of bare household-place nouns in 2026 is mined out.

Round 2 inverts the priority: **defensibility is the lead filter, evocativeness is the second.** That changes what kinds of names are in scope.

## New strategy

The candidate list below draws from four name types deliberately excluded the first time. Each type trades off differently:

**Compound words (real-word combinations).** _Hometable_, _Houseboard_, _Hometide_. Two real words fused into one new word. Pros: linguistically meaningful, easier to understand than a coinage, materially more defensible than a bare noun. Cons: longer wordmark, can read corporate if chosen carelessly.

**Coinages (made-up words).** _Kintly_, _Hofly_, _Maru_. The Slack / Notion / Asana / Stripe pattern — a short, pronounceable word that didn't exist before. Pros: maximum defensibility, the brand defines the word rather than competing for it, future-proof. Cons: requires brand-building work to fill with meaning, less immediately legible.

**Lesser-used English nouns.** _Stoop_, _Eaves_, _Steading_, _Toft_, _Curtilage_. Real words that exist but are uncommon enough to have clear namespaces. Pros: real meaning intact, much more defensible than common nouns. Cons: some need a tagline to land; some risk feeling archaic.

**Modifier-plus-noun phrases.** _Around Here_, _Common House_, _Family Roster_. Two-word names that read as a phrase. Pros: distinctive in app stores and trademark search, can encode positioning directly. Cons: cost in wordmark length, harder to type.

I'd lean toward **compounds** and **coinages** as the primary directions, with lesser-used English nouns as a secondary option for warmth.

## Candidate list

### Compounds

#### Hometable
**Why it works:** The literal place family business happens. Encodes the multi-person, multi-task nature of home-pm directly. Reads warm, sets cleanly in Fraunces.
**Tone:** Warm, gathered, plainspoken.
**URL ideas:** `hometable.app`, `hometable.family`, `gethometable.com`.
**Risks:** Not screened yet. "Home" + "table" is a common pairing in interior-design and home-decor blogs — may have SEO competition even if no direct app collision.

#### Houseboard
**Why it works:** Direct evolution of the current placeholder name ("The Board" → "Houseboard") without inheriting the genericism. Specific to a home context, distinctive, brand-able.
**Tone:** Plainspoken, slightly utilitarian, warm.
**URL ideas:** `houseboard.app`, `houseboard.family`, `gethouseboard.com`.
**Risks:** Not screened yet. Possible confusion with "houseboat" in voice contexts; "board" still carries some genericism even compounded.

#### Hometide
**Why it works:** Coinage-flavored compound that captures the _rhythm_ of household life — bills come in, chores cycle, kids grow. Fits the Routines feature unusually well. Distinctive, brand-able.
**Tone:** Literary, calm, slightly poetic.
**URL ideas:** `hometide.app`, `hometide.family`, `gethometide.com`.
**Risks:** Not screened yet. Lower-frequency compound, so less likely to collide; the literary register may not appeal to all audiences.

#### Familystand
**Why it works:** Compound, captures "where family business stands". Slightly utilitarian.
**Tone:** Earnest, plainspoken.
**URL ideas:** `familystand.app`, `getfamilystand.com`.
**Risks:** Not screened yet. Slightly clunky in a wordmark; might read more enterprise-HR than warm-household.

### Coinages

#### Kintly
**Why it works:** Built from _kin_ (family) plus an adverbial suffix. Sounds like a real word but isn't. Distinctive, brand-able, very defensible. Fits a multi-person household tool naturally.
**Tone:** Warm, modern, friendly.
**URL ideas:** `kintly.app`, `kintly.com`, `kintly.family`.
**Risks:** Not screened yet. Coinages need brand-building work to fill with meaning; not self-explanatory on first sight.

#### Maru
**Why it works:** Japanese word meaning "circle" or "complete" — perfectly captures a family unit and the closing-the-loop nature of task completion. Two syllables, soft, distinctive in English-speaking markets. Beautiful in Fraunces.
**Tone:** Calm, considered, slightly literary.
**URL ideas:** `maru.app`, `maru.family`, `usemaru.com`.
**Risks:** Not screened yet. Japanese-origin name in a Western consumer app is a real choice — appropriative concerns possible if not handled with care; also some risk of pronunciation drift in English ("mah-roo" vs "mar-oo").

#### Hofly
**Why it works:** Pure coinage. No prior meaning, fully defensible, gives the brand room to define itself entirely. Easy to say, easy to remember.
**Tone:** Modern, light, slightly playful.
**URL ideas:** `hofly.app`, `hofly.com`.
**Risks:** Not screened yet. "Fly" suffix may read tech-startup-y in a way that fights the warm editorial identity; doesn't carry any meaning yet, so tagline must do all the work.

#### Tendly
**Why it works:** Coinage from _tend_ — what families do for each other. Distinctive, easy to say, family-coded without being saccharine.
**Tone:** Warm, gentle, active.
**URL ideas:** `tendly.app`, `tendly.com`, `tendly.family`.
**Risks:** Not screened yet. May collide with dental-care or plant-care brands using "Tend". Less distinctive in voice than _Kintly_.

### Lesser-used English nouns

#### Stoop
**Why it works:** Short, single-syllable, American, evocative. The stoop is the front step where families and neighbors gather. Strong NYC / Philly / Brooklyn association in a way that could read as urban-warm, not folksy.
**Tone:** Plainspoken, urban-warm, distinctive.
**URL ideas:** `stoop.app`, `stoop.family`, `getstoop.com`.
**Risks:** Not screened yet. "Stoop" has been used in some media/publishing brands; possible Australian connotation ("Stoopid"). Pronunciation/spelling are fine.

#### Eaves
**Why it works:** The overhanging edge of a roof — a sheltering image. Short, single-syllable, soft. Less crowded namespace than _hearth_ or _roost_.
**Tone:** Warm, sheltering, slightly literary.
**URL ideas:** `eaves.app`, `eaves.family`, `eaves.house`.
**Risks:** Not screened yet. Single English word — must screen carefully. Sounds like "Eves" (name); could create confusion.

#### Steading
**Why it works:** Scottish English for a farmstead — the buildings on a working homestead. Semantically perfect for the product. Distinctive because uncommon. (Note: this is _not_ Stead — it's the noun form, less collided.)
**Tone:** Quiet, grounded, literary.
**URL ideas:** `steading.app`, `steading.family`, `steading.house`.
**Risks:** Not screened yet. Slight risk of pronunciation drift and confusion with the disqualified _Stead_. Less common word; needs tagline.

#### Toft
**Why it works:** Old English for a homestead with its surrounding land. Ultra-short, ultra-defensible (very few brands use it), real meaning intact. Beautiful in Fraunces.
**Tone:** Quiet, old, distinctive.
**URL ideas:** `toft.app`, `toft.family`, `toft.house`.
**Risks:** Not screened yet. Recognizability is low — most users won't know the word, so it functions almost like a coinage. Tagline must carry the explanatory load. Some risk of being mistaken for a brand name with no meaning.

### Modifier + noun phrases

#### Around Here
**Why it works:** Pulled directly from the existing UI placeholder ("What needs doing around here?"). Phrase-as-name reads conversational and warm. Distinctive in trademark search.
**Tone:** Conversational, slightly cheeky, warm.
**URL ideas:** `aroundhere.app`, `around.house`, `getaroundhere.com`.
**Risks:** Not screened yet. Two words is a real cost in wordmark; possible collision with location/neighborhood apps named "Around".

#### Common House
**Why it works:** Refers to a shared physical space in cohousing communities. Evokes the multi-household nature of home-pm directly. Reads warm and intentional.
**Tone:** Communal, grounded, intentional.
**URL ideas:** `commonhouse.app`, `commonhouse.family`.
**Risks:** Not screened yet. "Common" is a heavily-used word in tech brands (Common, Common Room, etc.).

## Recommended top 3 to screen next

Given the lessons from Round 1, I'd prioritize three names from different name-type buckets so the next screen also tells us what _kind_ of name is most ownable, not just which specific name passes:

1. **Hometide** — most distinctive compound, captures rhythm-of-household specifically, low-frequency word pairing.
2. **Kintly** — most defensible coinage, family-coded without being twee, easy to say.
3. **Toft** — most defensible English noun, real meaning, ultra-short.

If one or more of these clears screening, I'd default to the survivor. If all three fail, the pattern of name types still in play will inform the next round.

**Honorable mention for review:** _Maru_ is the most beautiful single-word option but the cultural-appropriation question makes it a non-trivial decision that's bigger than this screen can resolve. Worth a conversation, not just a search.

## Caveats

None of these have been screened. The list above is a candidate set, not a recommendation set. Same caveat as before applies: a proper attorney-led trademark search is required before any commercial commitment under any name.
